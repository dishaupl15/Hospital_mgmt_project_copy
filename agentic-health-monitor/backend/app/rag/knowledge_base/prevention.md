## CARDIOVASCULAR HEALTH

### Heart Disease Prevention
- Regular aerobic exercise: at least 150 minutes of moderate intensity per week
- Maintain healthy weight: BMI between 18.5 and 24.9
- Diet: low saturated fat, low sodium, high in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains
- Avoid smoking and limit alcohol to moderate levels
- Monitor and control blood pressure: target below 130/80 mmHg
- Monitor and control cholesterol: LDL below 100 mg/dL for high-risk individuals
- Regular blood sugar checks if overweight or family history of diabetes
- Manage stress through relaxation techniques, adequate sleep

### Stroke Prevention
- Control hypertension — the single most important modifiable risk factor
- Treat atrial fibrillation with anticoagulation as prescribed
- Quit smoking — doubles the risk of stroke
- Control diabetes with diet, exercise, and medications
- Regular check-ups for atrial fibrillation screening after age 65

## METABOLIC HEALTH

### Diabetes Prevention (Type 2)
- Achieve and maintain healthy body weight
- Regular physical activity: at least 30 minutes most days
- Diet: reduce refined carbohydrates and added sugars, increase fiber
- Prediabetes management: lifestyle modification can prevent or delay Type 2 diabetes by 58%
- Annual fasting blood glucose check for those over 45 or with risk factors
- Avoid tobacco products — smoking increases insulin resistance

### Managing Existing Diabetes
- Self-monitor blood glucose as recommended by healthcare provider
- Take medications consistently, do not skip doses
- Annual HbA1c testing to assess 3-month average blood sugar control
- Annual eye exam for diabetic retinopathy screening
- Annual foot exam — check for neuropathy, sores, and poor circulation
- Annual kidney function and urine protein testing
- Vaccinations: influenza, pneumococcal, hepatitis B as recommended

## LIVER HEALTH

### Hepatitis Prevention
- Hepatitis A: vaccination, hand washing before eating and after bathroom, safe food and water hygiene
- Hepatitis B: vaccination series (3 doses), safe sex practices, do not share needles or personal care items
- Hepatitis C: do not share needles, ensure safe blood transfusion practices
- Avoid excessive alcohol: safe limits are up to 1 drink/day for women, 2 drinks/day for men
- Avoid hepatotoxic medications in excessive amounts (e.g., paracetamol overdose)
- Maintain healthy weight to prevent non-alcoholic fatty liver disease

### Alcohol-Related Liver Disease Prevention
- Complete alcohol abstinence for those with existing liver disease
- Regular liver function tests for heavy drinkers
- Monitor for early signs: fatigue, right upper abdominal discomfort, loss of appetite

## RESPIRATORY HEALTH

### Asthma and Lung Disease Prevention
- Quit smoking — most important action for lung health
- Avoid second-hand smoke exposure
- Identify and minimize exposure to asthma triggers: dust mites, pet dander, mold, pollen, cockroaches
- Use air purifiers in bedroom for allergy-induced asthma
- Annual influenza vaccination — respiratory infections are the most common asthma trigger
- Pneumococcal vaccination for high-risk groups (elderly, immunocompromised, chronic lung disease)
- Avoid occupational exposures: use protective equipment around dusts, fumes, and chemicals

## INFECTIOUS DISEASE PREVENTION

### General Infection Prevention
- Hand hygiene: wash hands with soap and water for at least 20 seconds, especially before eating and after using bathroom
- Avoid touching face with unwashed hands — main route of respiratory virus transmission
- Cover coughs and sneezes with elbow, not hands
- Stay home when sick to prevent spreading infection to others
- Keep vaccinations up to date: influenza annually, tetanus every 10 years, COVID-19 as recommended

### Food Safety
- Cook meat and poultry to safe internal temperatures
- Separate raw and cooked foods to prevent cross-contamination
- Refrigerate perishable foods within 2 hours
- Wash fruits and vegetables thoroughly under running water
- Safe water: use treated or boiled water in areas with poor sanitation

### Travel Health
- Consult healthcare provider 6-8 weeks before international travel for recommended vaccines and prophylaxis
- Hepatitis A vaccination for travel to developing countries
- Malaria prophylaxis for travel to endemic areas
- Safe food and water precautions in areas with poor sanitation
- Dengue prevention: use insect repellent, wear long sleeves, eliminate standing water

## MENTAL WELLNESS

### Stress Management
- Regular physical activity reduces stress hormones (cortisol and adrenaline)
- Adequate sleep: 7-9 hours per night for adults
- Social connection and support networks reduce isolation-related health risks
- Mindfulness and relaxation techniques: deep breathing, meditation, yoga
- Limit caffeine and alcohol — both disrupt sleep and increase anxiety
- Seek professional support early for persistent anxiety or depression

## GENERAL WELLNESS GUIDELINES

### Recommended Screening Intervals
- Blood pressure: every 1-2 years for adults with normal levels; more frequently if elevated
- Blood glucose: every 3 years for adults over 45; annually if risk factors present
- Cholesterol: every 4-6 years for adults; more frequently if risk factors present
- Colorectal cancer screening: starting at age 45 for average-risk adults
- Cervical cancer (Pap smear): every 3 years for women aged 21-65
- Breast cancer mammography: annually or every 2 years from age 40-74 depending on risk
- Vision and hearing checks: every 1-2 years for adults over 50
- Dental check-ups: every 6 months

### When to Seek Routine Medical Care
- Annual physical examination with primary care provider
- Any new symptom that persists beyond 1-2 weeks
- Symptoms that interfere with daily activities or sleep
- Any symptom causing personal concern even if mild
- Medication review: annually or when starting new medications

### Warning Signs Never to Ignore
- Sudden unexplained weight loss of more than 5kg
- Persistent cough lasting more than 3 weeks
- Unexplained fever lasting more than 2 weeks
- Blood in urine, stool, or sputum on more than one occasion
- Persistent fatigue not explained by lifestyle factors
- Any new lump or thickening anywhere in the body
- Changes in bowel habits lasting more than 3 weeks
- Any mole changing in size, shape, or color
